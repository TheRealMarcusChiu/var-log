1. A straight [line segment](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/LineSegment.html) can be drawn joining any two points.
2. Any straight [line segment](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/LineSegment.html) can be extended indefinitely in a straight [line](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/Line.html).
3. Given any straight [line segment](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/LineSegment.html), a [circle](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/Circle.html) can be drawn having the segment as the [radius](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/Radius.html) and one endpoint as the center.
4. All [right angles](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/RightAngle.html) are [congruent](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/GeometricCongruence.html).
5. If two lines are drawn that [intersect](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/Intersection.html) a third in such a way that the sum of the inner angles on one side is less than two [right angles](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/RightAngle.html), then the two lines inevitably must [intersect](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/Intersection.html) each other on that side if extended far enough. This postulate is equivalent to what is known as the [parallel postulate](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/ParallelPostulate.html).

Euclid's fifth postulate cannot be proven as a theorem, although this was attempted by many people. Euclid himself used only the first four postulates ("[absolute geometry](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/AbsoluteGeometry.html)") for the first 28 propositions of the [Elements](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/Elements.html), but was forced to invoke the [parallel postulate](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/ParallelPostulate.html) on the 29th. In 1823, Janos Bolyai and Nicolai Lobachevsky independently realized that entirely self-consistent "[non-Euclidean geometries](https://mathworld.wolfram.com/Non-EuclideanGeometry.html)" could be created in which the parallel postulate <em>did not hold.</em> (Gauss had also discovered but suppressed the existence of non-Euclidean geometries.)
