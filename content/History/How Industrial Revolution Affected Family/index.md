# Industrial Revolution's Impact on the Family

- large changes in the family system, especially in the status and roles of women and children
- economy shifted from one based on agriculture and commerce to one based on factory production in towns and cities. This caused work to become something that was done outside the home to earn a wage to provide for the family
- family unit kept its economic role as a consumer, but lost it's role as a producer
- this caused changes in the family system and the development of the new industrial working class. Now, every family member, including children, began working in wage-based labor in the new factories
- Industrial Nuclear Family's notion of 'motherhood' was consider sacred and the primary role of women became the norm/ideal
- women were nurturers and worked at home and their husbands financially supported them
- compulsory education for children
- idealized notion of childhood as an "age of innocence"
- home now center of love and emotional contentment/consolement
- young people married early and moved away because they could support themselves
- 20th century: families became smaller as birth rates declined due to:
  - more expensive to send children to school
  - children were no longer an asset working in a farm
- marriage delayed until separate households could be afforded
- consumer family - husband the exclusive provider, the head of the household, and the link between the family and society. The wife was the homemaker for whom new products were manufactured to assist in creating a comfortable home
- women thought to be more gentler, patient, and loving than men which is why hey role was to be a wife, mother, and housekeeper
- this implied that women only reached their potential once they had children
- men perceived as aggressive, and though which made them more suited for the workplace
- children expected to play under supervision of the mother, attend school, and remain protected form the hard work of the adult world

# Introduction

- Burgess and Lock, in their book, The Family (1953) said that the family is moving fast or slowly ‘from institution to companionship
- pre-industrial revolution; it not only socialized the young, it provided much other training of the child as well. A boy learned his occupation by working with his father at a younger age
- agrarian society the kinship group maintained the aged, nursed the sick, buried the dead and provided for the mentally ill or physically crippled. It also ensured support for widows and orphans and made a place for stray cases of unmarried men and women. As a result, kinship became a most binding and obligatory relationship, regardless of personal feelings, requiring that family interests take precedence over individual one

###### Changing Authority Pattern

- male authority is declining in the modern family
- concepts of symmetrical family and egalitarian family are taking place of traditional patriarchal family
- this is stemmed from:
  - increased economic and political opportunities
  - young couples no longer rely on family elders for educating experience for themselves and to their children
  - dual-career marriages increased the attitude towards equality between married partners

###### Changing Status of Women

- rights of women became more recognized

###### Changing Economic Functions

- modern family no longer united by shared work on the farm
- family were your coworkers and your coworkers were you family
- it maintained unit of consumption instead of its role as a producer
- it is now united only by feelings of companionship, affection, and recreation

###### Free Choice of Mate Selection

- with assumed individualism created by industrialism, it encouraged the ideal of romantic love and free choice of mate
- young people began to give more attention to their own feelings, and less to parental wishes

###### Decline of Family Size

- most married couples want to have children, but economic considerations forced them to have smaller family with one or two children
- young people believe that reproduction is not the duty of all married couples
- children are no longer economically necessary , unlike in agrarian society
- couples prefer to have a car rather than a child these days

###### Changing Attitudes towards Sex and Marriage

- increased levels of sexual freedom:
  - pre-marital sex
  - extra-marital sex
- increased co-habitation
- because of this, new forms of families have emerged:
  - families of single parents
  - families of homosexuals

Declining Functions of a Family

- some socializing functions that have once been performed exclusively by the family is now out-sourced to external agencies:
  - child raising - nursery schools
  - education
  - occupational training
  - caring for the elderly, sick, handicapped - health institutions

- As the society is becoming more and more industrialized, parents can less and less transmit occupational succession to their children

- Motor cars became common in the 1920's and 1930's in the US and people did not 'commute' more than a few miles until the 1950's and the development of the suburbs in the US
