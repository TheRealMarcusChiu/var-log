Gladys Aylward, born in 1904 in London to working-class parents, was at first glance a normal girl.  She grew up working as a parlor maid for most of her young life. However one day, she attended a revival meeting at which the speaker talked of dedicating one’s life to Jesus and to ministry.  From that point on, she chose to serve God.  Soon after, she felt that God was not only calling her to serve, but to be a missionary in China.  At the age of 26, she enrolled in the China Inland Mission Center in London. However, she was kicked out of the program because she couldn’t pass her exams and therefore was deemed unready for missions.  This would be the first of many obstacles she would face in her struggle to reach China. 

When Gladys got kicked out of the Inland Mission Center, she was devastated.  She felt that her dreams were coming to an end before they ever began to take shape. But one day, as she was sitting in church, listening to some old lady babble on and on, something the woman said stuck out to Gladys.  The woman mentioned her missionary friend who resided in China and who was currently looking for an apprentice.  Gladys blurted out without a second thought, “That’s me!”  Aylward immediately started looking for jobs where she could earn enough to buy train passage.  Though the travel agent warned her that the cheapest place to arrive at her first destination, Russia, was smack in the middle of a war zone, she carried on.  

Despite the seeming improbability of Gladys’s goals, she made her way on a train to Chita, Siberia.  Along the way, the train filled more and more will soldiers and less with civilians. This pattern repeated with every stop until she was the only civilian left.  She was alone on a train, with hundreds of men who didn’t speak her language and who were also carrying guns.  If this weren’t enough, the train conductor insisted that she step off the train. She refused, stating that her ticket was for a destination that was closer to China, whereas the conductor wanted her to get off in Chita.  Upon arrival, she realized that it was not a wise decision to stay on the train, as another train would not pass by Chita for three whole weeks.  She was forced to walk 30 miles in the dark, backtracking in the snow to arrive back at the train station.  There, she was detained and left without food for three days.  

In Chita, the officers that detained her stole her passport and changed the word “missionary” to “machinist,” and forced her to go to a shady hotel where she was not allowed out of her room without supervision.  They were going to force her to work with machines in Russia forever.  One night, a young girl came to her with instructions on how to escape.  She followed these instructions and found herself at the docks of a seaport.  She boarded a ship that was bound for Japan and eventually found her way to Yangcheng, China through a series of unfortunate events.   

When she arrived in China, she traveled by mule two days to meet Mrs. Lawson, the woman whom she would be helping.  Mrs. Lawson was gruff but kind, and she put Gladys right to work.  Gladys soon discovered the name that all of the people of China called her: “white devil.” The first day she arrived, Gladys was attacked by the townspeople.  However, her and Mrs. Lawson had a goal.  Soon, they had turned a dilapidated old building into a hotel where muleteers could rest between stops. They named it the Inn of the Eight Happinesses (just because it sounded good.) There, as they were served dinner, the muleteers learned about Jesus.  Gladys and Mrs. Lawson would tell Bible stories to them as they ate.

One unfortunate day, when Gladys was out of town, Mrs. Lawson fell off of a balcony and was left in the courtyard for days with no help and no food.  Gladys arrived to find Mrs. Lawson barely alive.  Aylward did all she could but Mrs. Lawson passed away.  Gladys was now the only person in Yangcheng that could speak English.  She learned mandarin quickly, because she had no choice.  Soon, the leader of the region, called the Mandarin, requested Gladys’s help.  The Mandarin wanted Gladys to go around to all the small villages in the area and unbind little girls’ feet, because foot-binding had finally been outlawed.  Soon after Gladys fulfilled this task, the Mandarin called on her for everything, even breaking up prison fights.  Gladys became an invaluable resource for the Mandarin.  At the same time, Gladys was able to spread the gospel everywhere she went. 

In her travels, Gladys found that she encountered many orphaned children.  She took these children into the Inn to live there, basically adopting them.  The Inn of Eight Happinesses became an orphanage alongside an Inn.  There were so many children that needed shelter, it became primarily an orphanage. 

  As leaders in this region of China began to hear of Gladys’s works and progress, they began to be curious about this “God” that she claimed was inside of her spirit. The Mandarin himself, having seen the amazing things that Gladys could do through God’s strength, decided to become a Christian as well.  This overjoyed Aylward, because she felt that her calling was truly being fulfilled. 

One day, the children call her to the courtyard and point out planes in the sky.  The Japanese had come, and they began to bomb nearby places.  The Mandarin instructed her to take the children and trek across 100 miles to safety.  The journey was dangerous, painful, and exhausting.  Children were Gladys’s priority, and she rarely ate. There was hardly any food to go around, and the children grew tired often and quickly.  She tried to get the children to sing songs as they were hiking through the mountains, but they grew tired of singing.  She begged for God’s grace and provision, but she was getting discouraged quickly.  

The war raged on in China.  The Japanese were cruel and violent to families, women, and children.  They ravaged homes, invading and killing wherever they went.  Gladys had to take her children on a very long journey, much farther than she could’ve anticipated.  They ran into danger, hunger, sickness. They traveled on coal trains, hoping not to be bombed.  They were exhausted at all times. Yet Gladys pushed on.  Within two days of finally reaching an orphanage to keep all the children in, Gladys fell into a coma.  Nobody could wake her from it, and she was taken to a hospital. 

As she got stronger, she returned to missions, this time traveling to speak to monks about the Christian faith. Now, the major threat was the communists in China.  After the war with the Japanese ended, the Chinese themselves were fighting against each other.  The weakness from the war had allowed the Communist party to gain control in China. The Communists destroyed monasteries, churches, and any other religious establishments.  This made Gladys’s job even more difficult. 

Gladys kept on preaching the gospel, speaking to many young university students.  The time came for the new Communist government to post a mandatory survey to gather the general opinion of the people.  The “O” represented support of the new communist government, while the “X” represented opposition.  The government was infuriated to find that 200 university students marked “X’s.” 

As communism grew, more and more violence spread. At one point, there was a public beheading for anyone who was called to
the square that would not swear loyalty to Communism.  Gladys was devastated as she left that day; devastated at what China was 
becoming.  The violence eventually forced her to move to Shanghai, where the controversy was not as prominent.  It was getting too hard to do missions work in China. 

Eventually, she felt that her journey in China was coming to an end.  She boarded a train and headed back to London.  She lived there, traveling and spreading the gospel from time to time.  Her long life of missions had finally worn her out.  One night, she went 
to sleep and simply never woke up.  However, sleeping peacefully in a crib beside her was a baby she encountered who needed a home. Gladys Aylward dedicated her life to serving God and to helping others, even with her last breaths.
