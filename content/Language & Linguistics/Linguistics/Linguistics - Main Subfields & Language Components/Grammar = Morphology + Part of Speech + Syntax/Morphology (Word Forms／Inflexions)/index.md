# <strong>Morphology</strong>

###### Morpheme

- the basic unit of morphology
- morphemes are the smallest meaningful unit of language
- a morpheme is a series of phonemes that has a special meaning

###### Morpheme Types

- <strong>free morphemes</strong> -<strong> stems</strong> -<strong> root words</strong> - are the main morphemes of words
  - <strong>open class</strong> - verbs, nouns, adjectives, and adverbs
  - <strong>closed class</strong> - have a relatively fixed membership (conjunctions, prepositions, determiners, etc)
- <strong>bound morphemes</strong> - <strong>affixes</strong> - adds additional meaning to the stems to modify their meanings and grammatical functions
  - affix types (syntax placement)
    - prefix - placed in front of word
    - suffix - placed at end of word (e.g. <em>-fer</em>, <em>-late)</em>
    - infix - placed within a word
    - circumfix - placed in front and end of word
  - affix types (semantics)
    - inflectional affixes - changes the [[Verb (Tense & Aspect)|tense]] of a [[Verbs|verb]] or the number value of the [[Nouns|noun]]
      - <em>-s</em>, <em>-ed, -ing</em>
    - derivational affixes - changes the meaning or <em>[[Part of Speech (PoS)|part-of-speech]]</em> of the word
      - <em>-ness</em>, <em>un-</em>, <em>-ify, <em>-er, -able, -ation</em></em>

###### Ways to Combine Morphemes to Create Words

- <strong><strong>concatenative morphology</strong></strong> - concatenation of 2 or more morphemes ordered one after to form a new word (e.g. “door” and “doors”; “run” and “running”)
  - <strong>inflectional morphology</strong> - combination of word stem and inflectional affixes, resulting in SAME word class (e.g. -s for pluralizing nouns and -ed for past tensing verbs)
  - <strong>[[Derivational Morphology|derivational morphology]]</strong> - combination of word stem and derivational affixes, resulting in DIFFERENT word class (e.g. -ation for turning verb to noun, computerize to computerization)
  - <strong>compounding morphology</strong> - combination of multiple word stems ([[portmanteau - contraction]]?)
  - <strong>[[Cliticizational Morphology - Clitic - Cliticization|cliticizational morphology]]</strong> - combination of word stem and a clitic. a clitic is a morpheme that acts syntactically like a word but is reduced in form and attached to another word. (e.g. 've in word I've is a clitic)
- <strong>non-concatenative morphology</strong> - morphemes are combined in more complex ways (e.g. “foot” and “feet”; “freeze” and “frozen”)
  - <strong>templatic morphology</strong> or <strong>root-and-pattern morphology</strong> - Semitic Languages
