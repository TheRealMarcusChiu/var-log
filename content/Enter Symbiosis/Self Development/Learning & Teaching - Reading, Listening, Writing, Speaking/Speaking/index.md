> ###### Start
> 1. Do not start a talk with a joke
> 2. Promise - Tell them what they gonna learn at the end of your talk
> 3. Cycle – make your idea repeated many times in order to be completely clear for everyone
> 4. Make a “Fence” around your idea so that it can be distinguished from someone else’s idea
> 5. Verbal punctuation – sum up information within your talk sometimes to make listeners get back on
> 6. Ask a question - an intriguing one
>
> ###### Place & Time
> 1. The best time for having a lecture is 11 a.m. (not too early and not after lunch)
> 2. The place should be well-lit
> 3. The place should be seen and checked before the lecture
> 4. The place should not be full of less than half, it must be chosen according to the number of listeners
>
> ###### Tools For Teaching
> 1. Board – it’s got graphics, speed, target. Watch your hands! Don’t hold them behind your back, it’s better to keep them straight and use them for pointing at the board.
> 2. Props – use them in order to make your ideas visual. Visual perception is the most effective way to interact with listeners.
>
> ###### For Job Talk. Exposing, Slides
> - Don’t put too many words on a slide. Slides should just reflect what you’re saying, not the other way around. Pictures attract attention and people start to wait for your explanation – use that tip
> - Make slide as easy as you can – no title, no distracting pictures, frames, points, and so on
> - Do not use a laser pointer – due to that you lose eye contact with the audience. Instead, you can make the arrows just upon a slide.
>
> ###### Informing
> Show your listeners your stuff that is cool and interesting.
>
> You have to be able to:
> - show your vision of that problem
> - show that you’ve done particular things (in steps)
>
> All of that should be done real quick in no more than 5 min.
>
> Persuade your listeners you’re not a rookie (Prof. Winston contrived to do that from the very first seconds of his talk)
> ###### Getting Famous
> If you want to your ideas be remembered you’ve got to have "5 S"
> - Symbols associate with your ideas (visual perception is the best way to attract attention)
> - Slogan (describing your idea)
> - Surprise (common fallacy that is no longer true, for instance, just after you’ve told about it)
> - Salient Idea (not necessarily important but the one that sticks out)
> - Story (how you did it, how it works…)
>
> ###### How to End
> - Don’t put collaborators at the end, do that at the beginning
> - Questions are the worst way to end a talk
> - It’s good to end with a Contribution slide – to sum up everything you’ve told with your OWN decision
> - At the very end, you could tell a joke since people then will leave the event feeling fun and thus keep a good memory of your talk
> - "Thank you (for listening)" isn’t a good ending, it’s trite at least. You can end with a quote from a prominent person (my own knowledge), with a salute to people (how much you valued the time being here, the people over here..., “I’d like to get back, it was fun!”
# Maxims
- <strong>maxim of relation</strong> - be relevant to the topic at hand
- <strong>maxim of manner</strong> - be perspicuous, avoid obscurity, and ambiguity, be brief, be orderly
- <strong>maxim of quality</strong> - say only what you believe to be true and only what you have evidence for (justified)
- <strong>maxim of quantity</strong> - be as informative as required, no more no less

# General Framework

a general framework for the majority of speeches looks something like this:
- <strong>Opening</strong>, including a statement of the overall thesis
- <strong>1<sup>st</sup></strong> piece of supporting evidence and your analysis of it
- <strong>2<sup>nd</sup></strong> piece of supporting evidence and your analysis of it
- <strong>3<sup>rd</sup></strong> piece of supporting evidence and your analysis of it
- <strong>Conclusion</strong>, summarizing your analysis and re-stating your thesis

Or in the famous words of Dale Carnegie: <em>“Tell the audience what you’re going to say, say it; then tell them what you’ve said.”</em>
# 3 Fundamentals in Speaking
- <strong>focus</strong> - The trick lies in really <em>knowing</em> your subject matter — not just the topic in general, but what you want to say about it specifically
	- <strong>topic </strong>vs<strong> thesis</strong> - know the difference
		- build your argument to an inescapable conclusion
	- <strong>assembling evidence types</strong> (if possible use all types):
		- factual
		- anecdotal
		- expert opinions - neither factual nor anecdotal
- <strong>rhetoric</strong> - the art of effective/persuasive speaking/writing, especially the use of [[Figures of Speech (FoS)|figure of speech]] and other compositional techniques
- <strong>presentation</strong> - the physical appearance of your speech
	- <strong>pacing</strong> - the overall pacing of a good speech should be slightly slower than your conversational speech. A popular Western example is to speak as if you were reciting the “Our Father” or a similar ritual prayer. persuasive speeches tend to arc in their pacing (slow, faster, slow)
	- <strong>visual aids</strong> - includes your clothing, your gestures, and even your expressions, as well as any physical objects

# Speaking Well
1. <strong>Get Your Thinking Straight</strong> - the first rule of plain talk is to think before you say anything. organize your thoughts
	1. <strong>know what you say</strong> - say it in your head first, don't wing it otherwise you'll add unnecessary filler
2. <strong>Say What You Mean</strong> - say exactly what you mean
3. <strong>Get 2 The Point</strong> - effective communicators don’t beat around the bush. If you want something, ask for it. If you want someone to do something, say exactly what you want done
4. <strong>Be Concise</strong> - don’t waste words. Confusion grows in direct proportion to the number of words used. Speak plainly and briefly, using the shortest, most familiar words
	1. <strong>short and concise</strong> - if you could say it in fewer words and with simpler words without reducing the impact, do that
	2. <strong>use independent clauses, and avoid dependent clauses</strong> - you want every idea to stand firmly on its own. (e.g. “An independent survey showed that Mac users were happier overall than PC users” sounds stronger than “Mac users are happier than PC users, according to an independent survey.”)
5. <strong>Be Real</strong> - for maximum clarity, be natural and let the real you come through. You’ll be more convincing and much more comfortable
	1. <strong>give an example</strong> - no one likes to be instructed, but give them an example that they want to follow
6. <strong>Speak in Images</strong> - words that help people visualize concepts can be tremendous aids in communicating a message
	1. <strong>act out the words</strong> - embody the characters and act it out, not just say it
7. <font style="color: rgb(112,112,112);"><strong><font style="color: rgb(51,51,51);">Stop, Look, and Listen</font></strong> - </font>speaking is only half the process. To be a truly accomplished communicator, you must also know how to listen
	1. be ready to adapt your delivery on the fly as needed
	2. diverted attention can be pulled back, but only if you know why the crowd is diverted
	3. match your room’s energy level

# Practice: You'll Need More Than You Think
Practicing speeches feels intensely unnatural. It just feels awkward when you speak alone in your room or your office. Add to that an intense amount of self-consciousness — in good practice you are, after all, scrutinizing yourself for mistakes — and you can see why most people find rehearsing speeches to be a deeply embarrassing experience
# Extra
- WHAT you say is a lot less important than HOW you say it.
	- instead of trying to perfectly articulate your thoughts into the “right” words, you should focus your energy on being more expressive
- Above all, the most important thing is that you are conveying the emotions behind what you’re saying
- “At the end of the day people won’t remember what you said or did, they will remember how you made them feel.” – Maya Angelou
