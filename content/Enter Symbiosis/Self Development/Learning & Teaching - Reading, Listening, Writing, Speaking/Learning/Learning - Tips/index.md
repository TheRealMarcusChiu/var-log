1. <strong>Recall</strong>
   - after learning something, drop everything and recall what you learned
2. <strong>Feynman Technique</strong>
   - state the subject to be learned
   - write out explanation of the subject
   - whenever you get stuck go back to material and relearn it. then rewrite explanation WITHOUT source
   - simplify
3. <strong>Spaced Repetition</strong>
   - do it again

According to a [massive study published](https://journals.sagepub.com/stoken/rbtfl/Z10jaVH/60XQM/full) in <em>Psychological Science in the Public Interest</em>. Here's a breakdown of different learning techniques, roughly ranked from least to most effective:

- <strong>Re-reading</strong>: studying text material again after an initial reading
- <strong>Highlighting/underlining</strong>: marking potentially important portions of to-be-learned materials while reading.
- <strong>Summarization</strong>: writing summaries (of various lengths) of to-be-learned texts.
- <strong>Keyword mnemonic</strong>: using keywords and mental imagery to associate verbal materials.
- <strong>Self-explanation</strong>: explaining how new information is related to known information, or explaining steps taken during problem-solving.
- <strong>Elaborative interrogation</strong>: generating an explanation for why an explicitly stated fact or concept is true.
- <strong>Interleaved practice</strong>: implementing a schedule of practice that mixes different kinds of problems, or a schedule of study that mixes different kinds of material, within a single study session.
- <strong>Distributed practice</strong>: implementing a schedule of practice that spreads out study activities over time.
- <strong>Practice testing:</strong> self-testing, or taking practice tests on to-be-learned material.

# Learning Tips

- <strong>Testing effect</strong>. Test yourself with practice papers or questions you made. Study > Test > Test is better than Study > Study > Study. "Retrieving information from memory is a dynamic process that alters the subsequent state of the system." Testing yourself, which is, retrieval of information is different from restudying. Testing, is a memory modifier that makes the retrieved material more accessible and retrievable
- <strong>Spacing effect</strong>. Space your study sessions. Spacing > massed practice (aka blocked practice)
- <strong>Interleaved practice</strong>. Spacing helps you discriminate the similarities and differences between similar subjects, induce future relevant material.

One general idea about these 3 study techniques is that they contract to learners' idea of performance and what is best during learning. You might think you're doing poorly with these techniques, but actually, they help you with long-term performance and retention
