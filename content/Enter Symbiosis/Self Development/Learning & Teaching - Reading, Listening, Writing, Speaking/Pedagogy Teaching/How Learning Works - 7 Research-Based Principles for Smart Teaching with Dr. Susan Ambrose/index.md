<strong>1. Prior Knowledge</strong>
- inactive

- When we ask them do you know what it is, teachers often mean (What, How, and When to use) while students often think of what it is.

Cognitive Scientist's 3 Types of Knowledge
- Declarative - giving definitions
- Procedural - actually do what is needed to do
- Contextual - when to use the knowledge

<strong>2. Organization of knowledge</strong>
- Expert v Novices
	- experts/instructors have rich meaningful knowledge structures that supports learning and performance
	- novices tend to build sparse, superficial knowledge structures
- visual aids helps a lot

<strong>3. Motivation determines, directs, and sustains what they do to learn</strong>

Teachers Expound These to Students
- Expectancy
- Value

Expectancy and Value creates -\> Motivation -\> leads to goal directed behavior -\> supports learning and performances

<strong>4. Goal-Directed Practice coupled with targeted feedback</strong>
- tell them why practice helps

<strong>5. Developing Mastery of a Subject</strong>

Students must:
1. Acquire component skills
2. Practice integrating them
3. Know when to apply them

More expert you you become prone to unconscious competence, leads to:
- over-estimate what novices know and can do
- under-estimate how long novices will take
- do not see the steps/pieces novices must learn
- mis-predict where novices will have difficulty
- presume that novices do things the way we do

Stages of Learning a Subject
1. Unconscious incompetence
2. Conscious incompetence
3. Conscious competence
4. Unconscious competence

<strong>6. Students are not only intellectual beings but are social, emotional beings</strong>

<strong>7. Allow Students to become self-directed learners, students must learn to monitor and adjust their approaches to learning</strong>

> 

1. Access the task
2. Evaluate strengths and weaknesses
3. Plan
4. Apply strategies monitor performances
5. Reflect and adjust if needed
6. Repeat if needed

Teaching is a progressive development, to improve its ways in order
