> [!expand-ui]- Overview
> # Negative Effects of Loneliness & Social Isolation
> - social isolation among older adults accounts for 6.7 billion in excess Medicare spending annually
> - loneliness and isolation are associated with lower academic achievement
> - stress-related absenteeism attributed to loneliness costs employers an estimated 154 billion annually
>
> 2022 study found that only 39% of adults in US said they felt very connected to others.
>
> less than 20% of individuals who often or always feel lonely or isolated recognize it as a major problem
> # Social Connection - The 3 Vital Components
> - <strong>structure</strong>:
> 	- the variety of relationships (e.g. co-worker, friend, family, partner, neighbor, mentor)
> 	- the number of relationships
> 	- the frequency of relationship interactions
> - <strong>functions</strong> - the degree to which others can be relied upon for various needs (e.g. emotional support, mentorship, support in a crisis)
> - <strong>quality</strong> - the degree to which relationships and interactions with others are positive, helpful, or satisfying (vs. negative, unhelpful, or unsatisfying)
>
> Social connection:
> - is a continuum (not a dichotomy)
> - is dynamic (not static) over an individual's lifetime
> - absence of social deficits does not necessarily equate to high levels of social connection
>
> # Is Social Connection Declining?
> - family size and marriage rates have been in steady decline for decades
> - the percentage of Americans living alone has also increased decade-to-decade.
> - In 1960, single-person households accounted for only 13% of all U.S. households. In 2022, that number more than doubled, to 29% of all households
> - In 2018, only 16% of Americans reported that they felt very attached
>   to their local community
> - In 2020, only 47% of Americans said they belonged to a church,
>   synagogue, or mosque. This is down from 70% in 1999 and represents a dip below 50% for the first time in the history of the survey question.7

> # Groups at Higher Risk for Social Disconnection
>
> Studies find the highest prevalence for loneliness and isolation among people with poor physical or mental health, disabilities, financial insecurity, those who live alone, single parents, as well as younger and older populations
> - while the highest rates of social isolation are found among older adults, young adults are almost twice as likely to report feeling lonely than those over 65.
> - the rate of loneliness among young adults has increased every year between 1976 and 2019.
> - lower-income adults are more likely to be lonely than those
>   with higher incomes.
> - Sixty-three percent of adults who earn less than \$50,000
>   per year are considered lonely, which is 10 percentage points higher than those who earn more than \$50,000 per year.
>
> # Impacts of Technology on Social Connection
> - Nearly all teens and adults under 65 (96-99%), and 75% of adults 65 and over, say that they use the internet.
> - Americans spend an average of six hours per day on digital media
> - One-in-three U.S. adults 18 and over report that they are online “almost constantly"
> - the percentage of teens ages 13 to 17 years who say they are online “almost constantly” has doubled since 2015.
> - the percentage of U.S. adults 18 and over who reported using social media increased from 5% in 2005 to roughly 80% in 2019.
> - Among teens ages 13 to 17 years, 95% report using social media as of 2022, with more than half reporting it would be hard to give up social
> - media.
>
> Several examples of benefits include technology that can foster connection
> by providing opportunities to stay in touch with friends and family, offering
> other routes for social participation for those with disabilities, and creating
> opportunities to find community, especially for those from marginalized
> groups.
>
> Several examples of harms include technology that displaces in-person
> engagement, monopolizes our attention, reduces the quality of our interactions, and even diminishes our self-esteem.

> [!expand-ui]- How Social Connection Impacts Individual Health and Well-Being
> - Data across 148 studies, with an average of 7.5 years of follow-up, suggest that social connection increases the odds of survival by 50%
>
> ![[Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Social Isolation/how-does-social-connection-influence-health.png|500]]
> - adults across the globe rate their social relationships, particularly with family and close friends, as the most important source of meaning, purpose, and motivation in their lives

> [!expand-ui]- How Social Connection Impacts Communities
> <strong>community</strong> - is a group of people with a common characteristic
> - One recent study on community violence showed that a one standard deviation increase in social connectedness was
>   associated with a 21% reduction in murders and a 20% reduction in motor vehicle thefts
> - A three-year study of 26 cities in the U.S. found that those with the highest levels of resident attachment experienced the greatest growth in GDP during the study period
> - growing ideological divisions in America are fueling skepticism and even animosity between groups across the political divide
> - sentiments of enmity and disapproval between Democrats and Republicans more than doubled between 1994 and 2014
