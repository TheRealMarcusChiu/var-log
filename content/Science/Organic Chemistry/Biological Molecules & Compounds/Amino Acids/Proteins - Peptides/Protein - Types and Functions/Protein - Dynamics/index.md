[[Proteins - Peptides|Proteins]] are generally thought to adopt unique structures determined by their [[Amino Acids|amino acid]] sequences, as outlined by [Anfinsen's dogma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anfinsen%27s_dogma). However, proteins are not strictly static objects, but rather populate ensembles of (sometimes similar) conformations. Transitions between these states occur on a variety of length scales (tenths of Å to nm) and time scales (ns to s), and have been linked to functionally relevant phenomena

### Protein Dynamic Regions

1. [Local flexibility: atoms and residues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_dynamics#Local_flexibility:_atoms_and_residues)
2. [Regional flexibility: intra-domain multi-residue coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_dynamics#Regional_flexibility:_intra-domain_multi-residue_coupling)
3. [Global flexibility: multiple domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_dynamics#Global_flexibility:_multiple_domains)
   1. [Hinges by secondary structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_dynamics#Hinges_by_secondary_structures)
   2. [Helical to extended conformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_dynamics#Helical_to_extended_conformation)
   3. [Shear motions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_dynamics#Shear_motions)
   4. [Domain motion and functional dynamics in enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_dynamics#Domain_motion_and_functional_dynamics_in_enzymes)
