## <strong>Svante Arrhenius Definition of Acids/Bases</strong>

<strong>acid</strong> - a compound that increases concentration of H<sup>+</sup>/protons when in aqueous solution (or H<sub>3</sub>O/hydronium when H<sup>+</sup> bonds with H<sub>2</sub>O)
<strong>base</strong> - a compound that increases concentration of OH<sup>-</sup>/hydroxide when in aqueous solution
###### <strong>example strong acid - HCl</strong>

HCl(aq) + H<sub>2</sub>O(l) -\> H<sup>+</sup>(aq) + CL<sup>-</sup>(aq)
###### <strong>example strong base - NaOH</strong>

NaOH(aq) + H<sub>2</sub>O(l) -\> Na<sup>+</sup>(aq) + OH<sup>-</sup>(aq)
## <strong>Brønsted-Lowry Definition of Acids/Bases</strong>

<strong>acid</strong> - H<sup>+</sup>/proton donor

<strong>base</strong> - H<sup>+</sup>/proton acceptor
###### <strong>example strong acid - HCl</strong>

HCl(aq) + H<sub>2</sub>O(l) -\> H<sup>+</sup>(aq) + CL<sup>-</sup>(aq)
###### <strong>example strong base - NaOH</strong>

NaOH(aq) + H<sub>2</sub>O(l) -\> Na<sup>+</sup>(aq) + OH<sup>-</sup>(aq)

## <strong>Buffers</strong>
Most organisms, including humans, need to maintain pH within a fairly narrow range in order to survive. For instance, human blood needs to keep its pH right around 7.4

<strong>Buffers </strong>- solutions that can resist changes in pH, are key to maintaining stable H<sup>+</sup> ion concentrations in biological systems.
- when there are too many H<sup>+</sup> ions, a buffer will absorb some of them, bringing pH back up
- when there are too few H<sup>+</sup> ions, a buffer will donate some of its own H<sup>+</sup>ions to reduce the pH

Buffers typically consist of an acid-base pair, with the acid and base differing by the presence or absence of a proton (a <strong>conjugate acid-base</strong> pair). For instance, one of the buffers that maintain the pH of human blood involves carbonic acid (H<sub>2</sub>CO<sub>3</sub>) and its conjugate base, the bicarbonate ion (HCO<sub>3</sub><sup>-</sup>).

- if too many H<sup>+</sup> ions build up, the equation above will be pushed to the right, and bicarbonate ions will absorb the H<sup>+</sup> to form carbonic acid
- if H<sup>+</sup> concentrations drop too low, the equation will be pulled the left and carbonic acid will turn into bicarbonate, donating H<sup>+</sup> ions to the solution
