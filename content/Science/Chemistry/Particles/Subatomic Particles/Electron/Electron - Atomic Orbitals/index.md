The term <strong><em>atomic orbital</em></strong> refers to the physical region or space where the electron can be calculated to be present

Each orbital in an atom is characterized by a unique set of values of the three [quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number):
- n - the electron's [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy)
- ℓ - the electron's [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum)
- m - the electron's angular momentum [vector component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_component) (the [magnetic quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_quantum_number))

Each such orbital can be occupied by a maximum of two electrons, each with its own [spin quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_quantum_number) s.

The simple names <strong>s orbital</strong>, <strong>p orbital</strong>, <strong>d orbital</strong> and <strong>f orbital</strong> refer to orbitals with angular momentum quantum number <em>ℓ</em> = 0, 1, 2 and 3 respectively
