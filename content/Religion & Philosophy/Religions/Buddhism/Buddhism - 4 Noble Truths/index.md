## <strong>4 Noble Truths</strong>
- [[Buddhism - 4 Noble Truths]] - all life involves suffering
- [[Buddhism - 4 Noble Truths]] - suffering is caused by desire and attachment
- [[Buddhism - 4 Noble Truths]] - desire and attachment can be overcome
- [[Buddhism - 4 Noble Truths]] - the way to overcome them is by the eightfold path

###### Dukkha (truth of suffering)
- Three Main Kinds of Suffering
	- old age
	- sickness
	- death
- parallels 1 Corinthians 11:30 "...weak, sick, and fallen asleep"
- in contrast to Matthew 16:21-28
- The problem of suffering goes much deeper. Human beings are subject to desires and cravings, but even when we are able to satisfy these desires, the satisfaction is only temporary. Pleasure does not last; or if it does, it becomes monotonous
- parallels Ecclesiastes 5:10-17 Riches are Meaningless

###### Samudaya (truth of the origin of suffering)
- The Buddha taught that the root of all suffering is desire/attachment (tanha - specifically means craving or misplaced desire)
- tanha comes in 3 forms (also called '<em>3 Roots of Evil</em>' or '<em>3 Fires</em>' or '<em>3 Poisons</em>'):
	- ignorance/delusion (represented by pig)
	- greed/extreme-want (represented by rooster)
	- hatred/extreme-dis-want (represented by snake)
- The Buddha taught more about suffering in the [Fire Sermon (can't find a good link)](http://doc.marcuschiu.com/)

###### Nirodha (truth of the cessation of suffering)
- The Buddha was a living example that liberate oneself from desire/attachment is possible in human lifetime
- <strong>Nirvana</strong>
	- means extinguishing
	- attaining nirvana - reaching enlightenment - means extinguishing the 3 fires: delusion, greed and hatred
	- nirvana is a state of profound spiritual joy, without negative emotions and fears
- The Buddha discouraged his followers from asking too many questions about nirvana. He wanted them to concentrate on the task at hand, which was freeing themselves from the cycle of suffering. Asking questions is like quibbling with the doctor who is trying to save your life

###### Magga (truth of the path to the cessation of suffering)
- final Noble Truth is the Buddha's prescription for the end of suffering (called the Eightfold Path or Middle Way)
- the eight stages are not to be taken in order, but rather support and reinforce each other
- Eightfold Path or Middle Way
	1. samma ditthi (right understanding)
	2. samma sankappa (right intention)
	3. samma vaca (right speech)
	4. samma kammanta (right action)
	5. samma ajiva (right livelihood)
	6. samma vayama (right effort)
	7. samma sati (right mindfulness)
	8. samma samadhi (right concentration)
- the eight stages can be grouped into
	- wisdom
		- understanding
		- intention
	- ethical conduct
		- speech
		- action
		- livelihood
	- meditation
		- effort
		- mindfulness
		- concentration
- The Buddha described the Eightfold Path as a means to enlightenment, like a raft for crossing a river. Once one has reached the opposite shore, one no longer needs the raft and can leave it behind
