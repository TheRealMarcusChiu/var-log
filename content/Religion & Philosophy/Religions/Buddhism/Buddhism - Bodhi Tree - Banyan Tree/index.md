# Bodhi vs Banyan

The name Bodhi tree was given to a specific tree of the species <em>Figus religiosa (peepal tree) </em>under which, it is said, the Buddha first attained enlightenment. The name has subsequently been used for other members of the same species.

<strong>bodh gaya</strong> - means <em>realized</em> in Hindi (bodh - sense | gaya - style) is a religious site and place of pilgrimage associated with the Mahabodhi (maha - big) Temple Complex in Gaya district in the Indianstate of Bihar. It is famous as it is the place where Gautama Buddha is said to have obtained Enlightenment (Pali: bodhi) under what became known as the Bodhi Tree

A banyan is a tree in the same genus (<em>Ficus</em>) which starts as an epiphyte. It is often used to refer to the species <em>Ficus benghalensis</em>, the Indian banyan, but can refer to any of a number of other species, not including <em>Ficus religiosa</em>.
# Banyan Tree

The banyan tree is also referred to as a strangler fig

It generally starts growing in a fork of some other tree in a litter of rotten leaves and bird droppings, sending aerial roots to the ground. Once there, more roots are to follow and after quite a few years, the strangler fig uses the host tree as a support and virtually strangles it. These are the trees with such a tangle of aerial roots that eventually resemble the trunks, that the cavity inside is big enough to live in. They are common in tropics and sub tropics where there is enough moisture.

The bodhi tree is not as nasty as that, being solitary and giving plenty of shade. It also grows in similar conditions as the strangler fig.

<strong>Strangler fig</strong> is the common name for a number of tropical and subtropical plant species, including some banyans and unrelated vines, including among many other species:
- Ficus Altissima - Fig Depth
- Ficus Aurea - Fig Gold

# The Bodhi Banyan Bungle

Nowhere is the ignorance of and confusion about Buddhism better illustrated than in the widespread inability to distinguish between Bodhi trees and Banyan trees. I thought everyone knew that the Buddha was enlightened while sitting under the Bodhi tree, otherwise known as <em>assattha</em> in Pali, <em>asvattha</em> in Sanskrit, <em>bo</em> in Sinhala, <em>po</em> in Thai, <em>bawdi</em> in Burmese, <em>pipple</em> in Hindi, Bodhi Tree or Sacred Fig in English, <em>Ficus religios</em>a in botanical writings; and that a Bodhi tree is one thing and a Banyan is another.
God! They look different enough! The Bodhi tree has thin bright-green leaves with the characteristic long pointed tip (right picture) while the Banyan’s leaves are ovate/elliptic-shaped, thick and dark green (left picture). The fruit of the former is small and brown while that of the latter is large and purple. Their botanical names are distinct too; <em>Ficus religiosa</em> for the former and <em>Ficus bengalensis </em>for the latter. But most noticeable of all is that the Banyan puts forth numerous aerial roots which support its spreading branches and form accessory trunks, and the Bodhi does not.

Saying that the Buddha was enlightened under a Banyan tree is a bit like saying Jesus was born in a milk pail or that he was nailed to an octagon, that a water melon fell on Newton’s head, that Santa’s slay is pulled by aardvarks or that the jolly swagman jumped into a bath tub. On one hand it’s not really that important, on the other it shows a superficial and casual attitude to clearly discernable differences and easily discoverable facts. In 2008 <em>Time</em> ran an article on corruption at Bodh Gaya entitled ‘Big Trouble Under the Banyan Tree.’ The August 8th 2009 edition of the <em>Economist </em>commenced a column called ‘In the Shade of the Banyan Tree’ in which it stated that the Buddha was enlightened under a Banyan. An excellent ecological website called Eco India (‘brings you down to nature’) says that Bodhi is another name for the Banyan (‘just brings you down’). Another website, Science Museums of China, gives a picture of a Bodhi tree, has its correct botanical name, lists its proper colloquial names and then spoils it all by calling it a Banyan tree. The tourist website for Phimai in Thailand gives a slightly different version of the muddle, stating that the Buddha was enlightened while ‘standing’ under a Banyan tree. Wikipedia, as I have come to expect by now, also buys into the confusion, at least in its article ‘Banyan Tree’. Out of 40 websites I logged on to at random, 36 got it wrong.
Come on people! It’s not that difficult! If you can tell a reindeer from an aardvark you should be able to tell a Bodhi tree (top/left) from a Banyan tree (bottom/right).
